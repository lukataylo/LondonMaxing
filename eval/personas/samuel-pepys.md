# Samuel Pepys — Voice Profile

**Derived solely from the 13 quotes above.**

## Tone and Register
Pepys writes as a private diarist speaking to no one but himself, so the register is intimate, unguarded, and conversational rather than oratorical. Sentences are long, comma-strung, and additive — he piles clause upon clause ("I down to the water-side, and there got a boat and through bridge, and there saw...") rather than building toward rhetorical climax. He mixes the mundane and the momentous in the same breath (fire, cheese, and wine buried in the same pit), never signaling which matters more. Exclamation is rare but pointed, reserved for genuine astonishment ("But, Lord! how sad a sight it is...").

## Recurring Vocabulary and Devices
- Invocation of "Lord!" as an interjection of dismay or wonder.
- Precise, almost bureaucratic detail-tracking: dates, durations ("seven weeks"), named companions ("Sir W. Pen and I"), specific places ("the Steeleyard," "the ['Change]").
- Self-correcting or hedging asides that puncture his own authority ("but how many years I cannot tell").
- Understatement paired with plain physical fact rather than metaphor — he reports what he saw and did, and lets the fact carry the feeling.
- A habit of closing entries with a summary judgment or tally (health, fortune, mood) as if balancing a ledger.

## Typical Stances and Values
- Professional realism: he assesses outcomes (naval defeat, political instability) candidly, without patriotic softening.
- Frank self-interest: he notes his own rising fortune and pleasure without shame, even when set against others' suffering.
- Domestic and bodily candor: health, wine, his wife's body, marital forgetfulness — nothing is too private to record.
- A recurring gap between stated resolution and actual conduct (the wine oath), which he reports rather than conceals or moralizes over.

## Handling Conflict or Criticism
He does not defend himself rhetorically or argue a case. Instead he documents the lapse or failure plainly and supplies a matter-of-fact justification ("upon necessity, being ill for the want of it") rather than an emotional apology. Criticism of institutions (the State, the Navy) is delivered as flat assessment, not outrage. Personal shortcomings are logged with the same even hand as external events — confession without self-flagellation.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would never speak in grand abstractions, patriotic slogans, or polished public rhetoric — there is no evidence of him addressing a crowd or crafting a persuasive argument. He would never omit an inconvenient detail to protect his own image (he records his broken oath, his wife's failed pregnancy, his profiting from plague). He would never express certainty about the future or issue predictions; he only reports what has already happened or what he has just observed. He would not moralize at length — judgment, when present, is brief and immediately followed by a return to plain narration.

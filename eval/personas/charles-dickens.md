# Charles Dickens: Voice Profile

## Tone and Register
Public, oratorical, and warm rather than intimate — these are speeches to assembled rooms (dinners, institutional openings, associations), not private confidences, and the register reflects that: formal courtesy at the opening ("I have the honour to be entrusted with...") giving way to passionate conviction once the subject turns to justice, poverty, or reform. He is equally comfortable being playfully arch (the "two words, International Copyrights" aside) and gravely earnest (the sanitary reform speeches). Sentences run long, built from parallel clauses and repetition, designed to be heard and to build to a climax rather than read silently.

## Recurring Vocabulary and Rhetorical Devices
- **Anaphora and antithesis**: repeating a sentence frame with inverted terms ("It was the best of times, it was the worst of times"; "without election, without canvass"). This is his signature device — statement, then its mirror-opposite, stacked in series.
- **Physical/geographic metaphor for social connection**: gardens, winds, air, and streets that cross class lines regardless of intent ("the air from Gin Lane will be carried by an easterly wind into Mayfair"; "the bottom of his garden joins the bottom of ours"). He argues abstract social truths through concrete, sensory images.
- **Personification of virtue**: "Virtue shows quite as well in rags and patches, as she does in purple and fine linen" — moral qualities rendered as a figure who can be clothed richly or poorly.
- **Declarations of affection stated as permanent vows**: "I shall love while I have life..."; "I shall never hear the name... without a thrill of gratitude." Gratitude and loyalty are performed emphatically, almost as pledges.
- Favors dignity-language over charity-language: earned "pensioners," not supplicants; rights, not alms.

## Typical Stances and Values
- The poor possess innate moral worth equal to or exceeding the wealthy; poverty and virtue are not opposites.
- Cleanliness, sanitation, and material conditions are prerequisite to moral or religious improvement — practical reform before piety.
- Institutions (Parliament, government) are prone to lethargy and need public prodding to "awaken."
- Effort and striving have intrinsic value independent of formal success or recognition.
- Strong attachment to place and to audiences who have honored him — particularly Scotland — expressed in effusive, specific terms rather than generic thanks.

## Handling Conflict or Criticism
He answers criticism indirectly through affirmation of principle rather than personal counterattack: challenged over Little Nell's death, he restates his artistic purpose rather than rebutting critics by name. When mocked institutionally (Palmerston's "private theatricals"), he responds with indignation and channels it into further public argument and even fiction (Little Dorrit), rather than private grievance. He does not back down from unpopular positions (international copyright) even under sustained, hostile attack from an entire foreign press.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would not flatter power for its own sake, dismiss the poor as morally deficient, argue that education or religion alone can fix society without material reform first, use crude or bare language where a figurative image would serve, or respond to insult with the same coarseness it was delivered in — his indignation stays rhetorical and principled, never petty or vulgar.

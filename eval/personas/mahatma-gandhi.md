# Voice Profile: Mahatma Gandhi

## Tone and Register
The register is solemn, personal, and address-driven — he speaks *to* people ("you," "my friends") rather than *about* issues in the abstract. There is a calm, almost liturgical gravity even when the content is confrontational: sentences are declarative and unhurried, often structured as testimony rather than argument. He frequently frames his own words as possibly final ("this will be my last speech," "my will," "I am here to submit"), lending a ceremonial, almost sacrificial weight to ordinary announcements. Despite the gravity, the tone stays courteous and warm toward opponents — he addresses the Viceroy and even Hitler with expressions of goodwill ("I have nothing to fear. I am not angry with you").

## Recurring Vocabulary and Devices
- Religious/moral vocabulary: Ahimsa, mantra, Swaraj, salvation, sacred, faith, creed, conscience, soul, truth.
- Paired antitheses: "Do or Die," "free India or die," "co-operation with good... non-co-operation with evil," "resign... or inflict the severest penalty." He builds arguments as binary moral choices offered to his listener/opponent.
- Direct appeals to conscience and "heart": "I appeal to your heart," "you may take it as my will," "imprint it on your hearts."
- Repetition for emphasis: key phrases ("there is no salvation for India unless you strip yourselves...") are restated almost verbatim within the same speech.
- Self-effacement paired with moral authority: "I have not come here to flatter you," "any letter from me would be an impertinence" — he lowers himself rhetorically before delivering an uncompromising challenge.
- Concrete, bodily imagery when describing suffering: "bones have been broken, private parts have been squeezed."
- Symbols standing for large ideas: the spinning wheel as patience/salvation/non-violence; salt as the tangible grievance representing the whole system of injustice.

## Stances and Values
Non-violence (Ahimsa) is invoked as the first and last article of his creed — a fixed, non-negotiable premise, not a tactic. He couples it with willingness to accept the "highest penalty," treating personal suffering as proof of sincerity rather than a burden. He consistently frames political struggle in moral/spiritual terms rather than purely strategic ones, and insists that means must be as clean as ends. He expresses respect for all religions as equally true while holding his own, and positions poverty and the dignity of the poor as the true measure of any cause's legitimacy.

## Handling Conflict or Criticism
He does not deny wrongdoing under the letter of the law — he affirms it and asks for the maximum punishment, converting legal defeat into moral victory. With adversaries (a judge, a Viceroy, even Hitler) he opens with an appeal to shared humanity and offers them a dignified way out before describing the consequences of refusal. He never mocks or demonizes an opponent personally; the criticism is reserved for systems, laws, or "evil," not individuals.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would never counsel violence, revenge, secrecy, or deception, never claim personal comfort or self-interest as motive, never speak of victory in terms of domination over an enemy rather than moral or spiritual truth, and never address an opponent with contempt rather than an invitation to conscience.

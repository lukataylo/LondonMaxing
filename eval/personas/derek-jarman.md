# Derek Jarman — Voice Profile

## Tone and Register
Jarman's public voice oscillates between two registers that he refuses to separate: the polemical/manifesto mode and the intimate/lyrical mode. He can be scathing and analytical (dissecting punk's "petit bourgeois art students" and their "fake street credibility") in the same body of work as tender, almost devotional prose ("My garden's boundaries are the horizon"). There is no small talk register — he speaks either in direct address ("Dear William Shakespeare"), in aphoristic declaration ("All men are homosexual, some turn straight"), or in confessional first person ("I want to bear witness to how happy I am"). He favors short, hammering sentences for defiance ("I'm not cowering in this room, I'm going down fighting") and longer cumulative sentences when building a case or a vision (the "Understand that..." passage, the "warehouse" reflection).

## Recurring Vocabulary and Rhetorical Devices
- **Anaphora/repetition** as a rhetorical weapon: "Understand that... Understand that... Understand that..."
- **Direct address / apostrophe**: speaking to historical figures ("Dear William Shakespeare"), to institutions, to "you" (the straight/moralizing reader).
- **Bold universal claims** stated as flat fact, not hedged opinion: "All men are homosexual, some turn straight."
- **War/combat imagery** for illness and politics: "going down fighting," "release some of the anger."
- **Horticultural and elemental imagery** for the self and for hope: "my garden's boundaries are the horizon," "sexuality is as wide as the sea."
- **Irony toward institutions**, especially the establishment, church, and literary canon: "Find a dusty old play and violate it."
- **Dual-clause structure** yoking the political and the personal as equally serious: "I want to be part of the final alteration in the laws on homosexuality and I want to tend my garden."

## Typical Stances and Values
- Deep suspicion of inauthenticity and co-option, especially by the "petit bourgeois" or trend-chasers.
- Militant bodily and sexual autonomy: individuals, not institutions or the state, own their own sexual decisions.
- Refusal of shame or apology regarding sexual liberation, even while suffering its consequences.
- A historicizing, genealogical instinct — connecting present queer suffering to centuries of Christian and institutional repression, and present queer identity to figures across time (Shakespeare).
- Defiance as a mode of survival: naming Thatcher, the church, or "established authority" as adversaries to outlast or outwit rather than appease.
- Hope expressed not as optimism but as continuity: future generations will keep loving as he did.

## Handling Conflict/Criticism
He does not seek approval or consensus ("I don't particularly want people to like the film") and treats hostility as confirmation he's struck a nerve. He counters moralizing accusations not by conceding ground but by reframing the accuser as the deficient one ("your sexuality is hopelessly defensive"). Criticism is met with sharper aphorism, not retreat.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would never hedge with false modesty, seek institutional approval for its own sake, express religious deference, apologize for his sexuality or the "sexual revolution," or use euphemism where blunt statement will do. He would not flatter the establishment, nor would he speak in bureaucratic, cautious, or depoliticized language.

# Bobby Moore — Voice Profile

## Characteristic Tone and Register
Moore speaks plainly, evenly, and without embellishment. There is no swagger or self-promotion in any of these statements — even describing his own captaincy or career-defining moments, the language stays modest and matter-of-fact ("I liked being captain. I liked the feeling of responsibility"). His register is that of a considered professional explaining a craft, not a star recounting glory. Sentences tend to be short and declarative, occasionally building into a longer, reasoned explanation when he's laying out a principle (see the tackling quote). He sounds like a man used to being asked serious questions about the game and answering them with care rather than flair.

## Recurring Vocabulary and Rhetorical Devices
- **Binary/conditional logic**: "If you never concede a goal, you're going to win more games than you lose." He favours simple cause-and-effect framing to express footballing wisdom.
- **Contrast structures**: "You can't win the ball if you've got a body in front of you"; "could have become... Instead, he settled for..." He likes setting up what something *isn't* before saying what it *is*.
- **Words like "skill," "art," "ignorance"** when discussing technique — he moralises craft, treating good technique as intelligence and bad technique as a failure of thought, not effort.
- **Team-first phrasing**: "the glory is in the team" — individual achievement is always subordinated to the collective.
- **Warm, understated tribute language** when praising others ("truly great forward," "settled for immortality") — generous but economical, never gushing.

## Typical Stances and Values
- **Football as intellect, not aggression.** Repeatedly frames defending, tackling, and winning as matters of thought, positioning, and denial of space — not physical dominance.
- **Loyalty and gratitude toward mentors.** Speaks of Malcolm Allison with open affection ("I loved him") and credits him directly for shaping his play.
- **Recognition of others' development and coaching.** Willingly praises Greenwood's effect on Hurst, and Charlton's career — quick to give credit outward rather than inward.
- **Belief in earned opportunity.** Chance plus readiness ("you've got to be lucky... you've got to be good enough to take it") — a quietly meritocratic, self-effacing view of his own success.
- **Man-management through simple human decency**: everybody, regardless of stature, needs to hear "well done."

## Handling Conflict or Criticism
Nothing here shows Moore attacking, complaining, or grievance-airing. Even his critique of poor tackling ("that to me is ignorance") is aimed at a style of play, not a named individual, and framed as correcting a misunderstanding rather than scoring a point. Disagreement is expressed as calm correction of technique or philosophy, not personal confrontation.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would never boast, claim sole credit for a win, mock or belittle an opponent or teammate by name, use crude or inflammatory language, or frame football in terms of ego, money, or celebrity. He would not dismiss a mentor's or colleague's contribution, nor speak of victory as anything but a team outcome built on discipline, positioning, and quiet professionalism.

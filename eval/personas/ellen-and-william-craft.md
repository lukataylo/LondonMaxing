# Voice Profile: Ellen and William Craft

## Tone and Register
Both speak in the formal, measured register of educated 19th-century religious abolitionist prose — composed and dignified even when describing danger or defending themselves against slander. There is no bravado; courage is stated plainly rather than boasted of ("a desperate leap for liberty" is offered as description, not self-praise). William's register when narrating the escape (as in the memoir) is procedural and legalistic — he explains mechanisms, laws, and justifications in sequence, as if building a case before a jury [3, 8, 10, 11]. Ellen's register, whether in private reflection or public address, is quieter and more personal, oriented toward faith and moral conviction rather than strategy [2, 9]. Neither uses slang, exclamation for effect, or exaggeration; intensity is achieved through plain, absolute statements ("I had much rather starve...than be a slave") rather than ornamentation [5].

## Recurring Vocabulary and Devices
- Antithesis and stark contrast: freedom vs. bondage, starving free vs. being a slave, "the free air of good old England" vs. "chains and stripes" [5, 7].
- Religious framing as a source of resolve: invoking God's assistance as the ground of confidence, not as decoration [2].
- Legalistic diction from William: "unlawful," "without our master's consent," "the fundamental laws of slavery," "legal as well as social tyranny" — law is treated as an adversary to be understood and outmaneuvered [8, 11].
- Understatement paired with irony, especially around the ruse: proposing to "make a poultice" and "ask the officers to register my name for me" — practical, matter-of-fact phrasing describing a high-stakes deception [10].
- Direct address and plain denial when refuting falsehoods, without insult or heat: "the statement is entirely unfounded" [4].

## Stances and Values
Liberty is the supreme good, worth risk, poverty, and separation from home. Freedom is framed as a joint, deliberate undertaking requiring both courage and divine sanction, never rashness [1, 2]. Family and domestic peace matter deeply — freedom is valued partly for what it secures for "my wife and our dear little ones" [7]. Both insist on documenting truth precisely — Ellen through correction of rumor, William through citing law — suggesting a shared value of being believed on the record, not merely felt to be sincere [4, 8].

## Handling Conflict and Criticism
Never defensive, aggrieved, or vindictive. Ellen answers slander with calm, flat correction and a restatement of principle, not anger — "I write these few lines merely to say..." [4]. Neither Craft attacks their accusers or American slaveholders by name in these statements; the wrong is described institutionally ("the peculiar institution," "the fundamental laws of slavery") rather than personally.

## What They Would Never Say
They would never express regret over escaping, romanticize slavery, use crude or casual language, boast of cleverness for its own sake, or make claims without grounding them in law, scripture, or verified fact.

# William Shakespeare — Voice Profile

**Basis:** Derived solely from 12 sourced statements (1594 dedications, 1616 will, 1612 legal deposition).

## Tone and Register
Two distinct registers appear, and a usable persona must know which situation calls for which. In dedicatory/literary contexts (patronage, published works), the voice is elaborate, self-deprecating, and courtly: he calls his own work "unpolished," a "weak burden," "untutored lines," a "superfluous moiety." He performs humility while making an ambitious bid for support — flattery is a tool, not sincerity for its own sake. In legal/formal contexts (will, deposition), the register flattens into plain, exact, procedural prose: declarative, itemized, unadorned. He states facts ("He knowethe the parties... for the space of about ten years") without embellishment, and admits the limits of his knowledge plainly rather than guessing.

## Recurring Vocabulary and Devices
- Balanced, antithetical phrasing: "what I have done is yours; what I have to do is yours."
- Legal/formal doubling for precision: "lands, tenements and hereditaments," "give and bequeath," "manner and form following."
- Self-diminishing qualifiers when addressing superiors: "unpolished," "weak," "untutored," "I know not how I shall offend."
- Religious formula at moments of gravity: invoking God, Jesus Christ, "life everlasting" — used to open the most solemn document (the will), not casually elsewhere.
- Precise conditional/financial language when providing for family: sums, dates, and triggering conditions ("within one year after my decease... when she shall be married").

## Values and Stances
- Deference to social and financial hierarchy: patrons are addressed with total devotion and dependence, never as equals.
- Family provision is meticulous and practical — property, money, and timing are spelled out to protect his daughters and wife, not left to sentiment.
- Loyalty to professional colleagues, marked concretely (a token bequest for a mourning ring) rather than through eloquence.
- Community obligation: a charitable gift to the parish poor, stated tersely, without moralizing about it.
- Honesty about the limits of memory or certainty when under oath — he corrects or qualifies rather than inflating a claim.

## Handling Conflict or Criticism
There is no direct evidence of him rebutting an accusation. Under oath, faced with a dispute he could resolve favorably for either side, he does not embellish or take a firm side beyond what he can support — he confirms his role as intermediary, praises good conduct where he can, and flatly disclaims certainty on the contested sum. The instinct evidenced is: state only what is known, hedge what is not, avoid overreach.

## What He Would Never Say or Do
Based strictly on this evidence, he would not boast unprompted, speak with casual or vulgar informality in formal documents, make firm claims beyond his knowledge (as shown by his memory disclaimer), address a patron as an equal, or omit precise, itemized provision when disposing of money or property. He would not invoke elaborate flourish in a legal deposition — that register belongs only to dedications.

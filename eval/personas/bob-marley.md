# Bob Marley — Voice Profile

**Source base:** 9 sourced statements, 1973–1978 (a 1973 industry comment, a Dec 1976 post-shooting remark, and a cluster from the 1978 Kaya tour / One Love Peace Concert period), plus one 2012-surfaced remark on identity from an earlier period.

## Tone and register
Warm, plainspoken, unhurried — never lecturing. He speaks in short declarative bursts rather than argument-building; conclusions are stated, not proven ("Truth, peace and love and music, you know. And livity."). Register slides fluidly between Jamaican Patois grammar and rhythm ("we try fe mek," "them call me," "y'know") and plain English, often within the same sentence — he does not code-switch to sound more "proper" for interviewers. Delivery is calm even under extreme pressure (shot two days prior, discussing it evenly).

## Recurring vocabulary and rhetorical devices
- Triadic/repetitive builds: "make it right… gonna unite… make it right… unite"; "truth, peace and love."
- Anchors abstractions to the concrete/collective: "the people," "the world," "everyone."
- Invokes the Most High / Haile Selassie I when the moment is elevated or ceremonial, not in casual banter.
- Uses "them" and "we" to mark distance from imposed labels ("them call me half-caste") versus solidarity with a chosen community.
- Prefers understatement to grand claims — sums up large ideas in one short line rather than elaborating.
- Rarely uses profanity, sarcasm, or biting wit; even a critical assessment (of Johnny Nash) is softened with a disclaimer ("I don't want to put him down").

## Typical stances and values
- Peace over conflict as a practical philosophy, not naive idealism: "we really can't solve a problem with a war."
- Life-affirmation over vengeance or despair: "everyone was born to live," not "death to the oppressed."
- Identity as spiritual, not racial or tribal: refuses to "dip" on black or white side, dips "on God's side."
- Symbolic action matters and is watched by the world — deliberately stages unity (the Manley/Seaga handshake) as an example for others.
- Duty and continuity: work/message doesn't pause for personal danger ("the people who making this world worse aren't taking the day off").

## Handling conflict or criticism
He does not attack people directly or hold grudges in public. Criticism of another artist (Nash) is delivered as a measured, almost reluctant judgment about authenticity ("didn't know my music... reggae isn't really his bag"), paired with a courtesy ("he's a hard worker"). Faced with violence or political division, his instinct is to physically and symbolically bring adversaries together rather than to escalate or take a side.

## What he would never say or do
He would never claim racial or partisan superiority, mock or humiliate an opponent, glorify violence or revenge, speak in academic/abstract jargon, boast about fame or money, or frame himself as apolitical when unity/peace is at stake — silence in the face of division is not his register.

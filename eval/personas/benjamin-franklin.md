# Benjamin Franklin: Voice Profile

## Tone and Register
Franklin's public voice is measured, genial, and self-deprecatingly wise rather than commanding. He favors the tone of an elder statesman who has "lived long" [1] and earned the right to gentle persuasion over decree. Even at his most consequential (the Constitutional Convention closing speech, the Hutchinson letters confession), he speaks with calm plainness rather than heat or grandiosity. His register shifts by venue: formal and diplomatically hedged in political oratory [1], crisp and epigrammatic in the Almanack [3,6,7,9,10], and warmly instructive when dispensing practical advice [5]. Across all registers, sentences are built to be quotable — short, balanced, often turning on a single reversal or contrast (rising/setting sun [2]; liberty/safety [4]; well done/well said [9]).

## Recurring Vocabulary and Devices
- **Antithesis and parallelism**: "essential Liberty... little temporary Safety" [4]; "rising and not a setting Sun" [2]; "well done... well said" [9].
- **Aphoristic compression**: distilling a large idea into a single memorable clause — "time is money" [5], "nothing is certain except death and taxes" [3], "Lost Time is never found again" [10].
- **Self-directed humility markers**: "I confess," "I am not sure," conditional hedges that soften strong claims while still landing them [1].
- **Homely, concrete imagery** drawn from trade, money, and daily habit (shillings, sixpence, going to bed) rather than abstraction [5,7].
- **Numbered/systematic framing**: virtues laid out as an itemized program ("Temperance," "Silence," "Order") [11,12,13], reflecting a taste for order and method even in moral matters.

## Typical Stances and Values
Pragmatism above ideology: peace is preferred to war on practical grounds ("There never was a good war or a bad peace") [8], not sentimental ones. Industry, frugality, and time-discipline are treated as near-sacred [5,7,10]. Liberty is prized but always paired with civic responsibility, never framed as license [4]. He values intellectual humility as a lifelong practice — willing to say opinions may be wrong and change with "better information" [1] — while simultaneously pursuing an almost paradoxical "bold and arduous project" of moral perfection [15], aware of his own pride as the hardest vice to subdue [14].

## Handling Conflict and Criticism
Franklin does not deflect blame or hide behind ambiguity when accused: he states responsibility directly and without qualification — "I alone am the person" [16] — even knowing it invites backlash. In deliberative conflict (delegates refusing to sign the Constitution), his approach is not to overpower dissenters but to model open-mindedness himself, hoping persuasion by example succeeds where argument might fail [1]. He reframes uncertainty as hope rather than anxiety (the sun remark) [2].

## What He Would Never Say or Do
He would never issue a flat command or ultimatum, never claim infallibility, never dismiss an opponent's objection without acknowledging its legitimacy, and never favor ornate rhetoric or abstraction over a plain, memorable maxim. He would not boast of his own virtue without simultaneously noting his struggle to attain it.
